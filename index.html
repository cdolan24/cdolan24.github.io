Writing


I've always had an interest in creative writing. When I was a kid, I spent much of my free time writing stories of varying quality but immense length. When the Covid-19 pandemic hit, I began to write with partners, manifesting myself as characters I wrote and spent hours, days, weeks, writing long stories about them. These characters would ultimately be the inspiration for what I animated later on, but developing them through stories and scripts would be especially important for building my game design knowledge. This character-centric style of writing and partner collaboration was also a gigantic influence on how I would later design games. The games I write are always centered around the characters, the players, and how they can interact with these designed fictitious worlds.


Another big influence for me was Tabletop RPGs, or TTRPGs. Games like Dungeons and Dragons, Pathfinder, and especially GURPS are something that I've been playing for over a decade. I've been to conventions, designed homebrew for my characters, done the whole nine yards. I have dozens of D and D characters, but anyone can do that. What was important for me was when I applied the character-centric format to other things. Breaking free of the rules of other systems and creating my own, I wrote more character sheets than I could keep track of. But that quickly graduated into creating my own game system around what I knew.


Night City Legends is a text based RPG system that I wrote in inspiration from the Cyberpunk 2077 videogame (or at least what the game was supposed to be) and the TTRPG it was based on, Cyberpunk 2020. The game is played on a Discord server that I created and appointed moderators to, but the important part of the server is the character creation system. Like most TTRPGs, Night City Legends revolves around building a character sheet with attributes and equipment and going on adventures in the world of Cyberpunk, making rolls for your abilities and installing cyberware to make your character more powerful. Each time you add augmentations to your character, though, you get one step closer to going insane, which is vitally important for the themes of madness and body augmentation in the Cyberpunk IP. Night City Legends, my character-driven RP system, has been playtested to some extent and remains wildly fun.


Animation


Before I knew I wanted to make a game, I thought that the best way to tell some of the stories from one of the reoccuring worlds from my short stories would be through animations. Unfortunately, I had no idea how to do this. My first foray into digital storytelling was through Blender's 3D modeling program with a fully homemade and home-rigged model I called "Danny the Default Man", who I modeled a body and clothes for by hand (and it shows).


Having completed my model after a few weeks of bashing my head into the wall, I decided to try animating this character. Unfortunately, it's now time for you to meet a good friend of mine, Marvin. Marvin is my computer, my darling, my child, and my partner in crime. Unfortunately, Marvin does not really do so well when it comes to... performance, which is something that a computer might need when trying to render something like, I don't know, animations.


In trying to animate Danny the Default Man on his very first flight, I spent weeks wrestling with my computer's poor performance and my lack of skills in both animation and optimization at the time. The finished render was all of one frame per second (that is not a joke, the actual number hovered between about 0.8 and 1.1) and was generally a tragedy. That being said, I learned a lot from this first animation, as you do when you're doing anything for the first time. I don't have that particular video any more, but ultimately I ended up doing a lot more animations to get my legs under me.


When I began work on my IB Personal Project “Inhuman,” I wanted to learn at least a little bit more animation for some gameplay. This prompted me to approach my Cartoon Animation teacher and ask if I could work on 3D animations in class, which she agreed to facilitate. I launched into a small spree of animations with the default Adobe Y-Bot both in and out of class to hone my skills, to varying degrees of success.


Some of the projects I've done were 24 hour challenges (though the time limitations were somewhat soft) where I would try to make the best animation possible within the set amount of time. This was especially convenient so I didn't spend too much time working on one thing and instead could move on to the next project (or homework). You can definitely see some of the performance issues in the computer and rendering.


I also spent some time working with static images and a bit of rendering to make sure I was, for the most part at least, not the cause of some of the rendering issues on other projects.

Computer Science


Computer Science. The life blood of video games. This may not have been the first way that I entered the game development cycle, but it quickly became the way that I intend to continue up through the industry. When I first began to work on my project Inhuman , which became a bit of a flop for a variety of reasons, I spent a lot of my time coding features rather than working on things like animations, which was what got me into the project originally. My focus on Computer Science in the last two and a half years is prevalent, and I'd like to walk through it a little bit.



When I was first writing text-based game systems on Discord I thought it might be interesting to have a native command for many of the randomized functions that you would be doing in the game. Not finding any way to do this that already existed, I decided to create a Discord chatbot that could handle some of these functions. For development purposes, the bot was split into two different entities, Tempobot and Scavengerbot. My father, a mathematician and general computer-science man, walked me through some Python to get started. The project worked, albeit with a fair amount of bugs, but to keep functionality and development active would require a server that I am not currently funding.


The idea was essentially to maintain character sheets and system rules inside the bot, so the the system and characters could be distributed across different communities who might want to use the game system for their own purposes. The bot could create random names, character greetings, general character descriptors, and allow you to search "chests" for random drops. Many of the TempoBot functions were largely social as well at the request of many of the people playtesting my system.




Another small project I worked on was very different in nature. I am an athlete in both Distance Track and Field and Cross Country and while my sport doesn't require much more than a good pair of running shoes, I still found myself repeatedly frustrated by the nature of many runkeeper apps. One of the ways we train is by doing repeats, which takes a few different forms, but the formula is largely the same. The issue I encountered was simply human error; I always forgot to turn off my watch and check the time. So I thought "what if there was a way to automatically stop the watch when you finished your lap?" This launched a very quick design process of a Samsung smartwatch application that I called "Splitkeeper". Splitkeeper was written on a platform called Tizen, which is designed exclusively for Samsung smart-device development, like TVs and wearables, which serves me because my devices are primarily Samsung. Unfortunately, Tizen has the kind of documentation and functionality that makes it... lets say "known" among programmers, so the project has moved at a snail’s pace as I research alternatives and as of right now is on indefinite hard-stop until my watch gets fixed. It was broken during a Cross Country meet and I have been unable to fix it as of right now.

    • Inhuman



    As a 10th grader at an IB school, one of our big culminating tasks is to do something called a Personal Project. While many people chose to take less than 10 hours to complete the whole project, I decided it would be the perfect platform to try and develop my first game. I had done a fair amount of animation and writing beforehand, and I wanted to move up on the interactive media tier list.



    Inspiration and Direction

    Inhuman was narratively inspired by many of the short stories I had written over COVID. The story would revolve around a group of superheroes, which would allow me to take heavy inspiration from one of my favorite games; Sucker Punch’s inFamous Second Son. The game would revolve around you, the player, who could pick from a wide range of superpowers to make the controller fit to your playstyle and go through a story in a massive open world. The game design document was full of mechanics to add and balancing notes, but also focused heavily on narrative. This narrative focus caused me to delve deep into my knowledge of comics and justify everything that happened in the world, which created a character-driven story around the player, exactly what I was looking for.

    Issues

    Inhuman suffered from something called scope creep. In trying to create my dream superhero game, I decided to add every mechanic that I thought might make the game greater with no regards of if I could handle creating something like that by myself in the single year that the personal project allowed for. Some of the features I intended to implement were multiplayer, a massive open world, and superb visuals. Rather than trying to come at the project from different angles and build up gradually, I stalwartly refused to take the "easy" (smart) way through the problem, which of course created problems. Messy projects, poorly written scripts, corrupt files, you name it, all of this happened during the Inhuman development. Given that my grit had served me well during my nightmarish COVID-19 experience, I trekked on, mistaking the resolve it had taken to get through something outside of my control for the stubbornness it takes to not change course when the answer is wrong.







    Lessons

    Inhuman is an experience that I still draw upon today. Its referenced all over my portfolio, and why would it not be? It taught me about computer science, game writing, production, social media presence and project management, probably more than any other project I’ve done. However, the game didn’t do everything poorly. The narrative focus of the game caused me to document all of the story and gameplay elements in a massive Google Site, which contained everything from major locations to main story missions similar to that of inFamous, each of which was complete with character sheets and powers (movesets) for many of the enemies. These character sheets and designs were massive and detailed, and all told I wrote enough to classify the story as a small novel.



    COVID-19

    My COVID experience was one that revolved around technical difficulty. By the end of the first semester, it was clear that this experience would affect my GPA much later on in life. While I produced okay work, many of my assignments would not submit or deleted constantly, meaning I had to redo dozens upon dozens of assignments by myself. I had no communication with other classmates considering I had come later than everyone else and only been at the school for a few months. They meant well, but my teachers eventually got sick of hearing my saga, used to dealing with other 9th graders who would lie about something similar. To even get through, I had to muster an insane amount of grit and faith that everything would work out as my counselors carried me across the finish line, my segment of the gradebook ridden with the bullet holes of digitally shredded assignments. I took time to make sure I understood the material, but it will never be reflected.



    Space Toboggan


    Overview - Space Toboggan


    Having recently learned what scope creep is, my goal in designing whatever my next project might be would have to revolve around simplicity. Rather than designing a tiny project, I decided to build something that would start off small with the potential to get bigger as my skills grew. The final game could be released with polished systems thanks to development in smaller projects. This approach to breaking the larger project into smaller games would be very helpful in my learning later on.


    Inspiration - Artemis


    The concept for Space Toboggan comes from two of my favorite games of all time. Artemis Spaceship Bridge Simulator is a game I picked up at a Dungeons and Dragons convention. The game consists of a group of people on local multiplayer, ideally played in a large room like how it was structured at the convention, with each person adopting a role on a spaceship similar to Star Trek. The result is a bunch of people all shouting across the room to each other to work in symbiosis to execute trades, engage in combat, and fly through the stars. As I played, I watched the ship creep across the stage, oftentimes having nothing to do in a specific role. I wondered if there was a way to cut out the fat to make a more engaging experience.


    Inspiration - Tempest


    The other massive influence was an arcade game from the 80s, Tempest. Tempest has been perhaps my favorite arcade game of all time since I first found a cabinet in elementary school. I actually played so much that I would get into high score competitions with my boss while I worked in the comic shop. I bought a Tempest shirt and wore it until it was destroyed. I love the game, and I know it well. To explain, Tempest is a shooter game where the player looks down a tube and has to shoot incoming enemies. It's a fast paced game, and when thinking about ways to counter the slow aspects of the Artemis experience, it wasn't long before I thought of Tempest.



    Approach


    Before getting too far down the design pipeline, I knew that I had to keep a firm grip on scope before repeating the same mistake I had with Inhuman. I also felt that creating such a massive game would destroy the arcade origins of the design (part of the reason I incorporated them!) So, each step of the way in designing this game, I asked myself how feasible it was for me to actually program this project. I looked up specific YouTube tutorials and documentation to make sure that there would be resources I could use for each feature I decided to code in the game. Even if the game was a short arcade-style game, I still was extra cautious with the design. To make sure that my goals were attainable, I split the project (which I called "Space Toboggan" as the design process started and it just sort of stuck) into three smaller games.


    Chute

    To approach the piloting aspect of the game, I designed a very small game called “chute” which had a little pilot controller that careened down a procedurally generated tunnel (a chute). This was to prove that I could build a good piloting system and use some procedural generation elements that would come in handy for the arcade elements.


    Spinner

    To approach the second role, the gunner, I decided to build a game where you sat in a turret and shot at objects in the sky. You could spin the turret around the base for different aim, a proof of concept for the idea I had for the gunner seat where you did something similar attached to the pilot seat.


    Character Selector

    The third “game” I sketched was much less flamboyant but one of the most technically challenging. In order to understand how to make and manage a multiplayer class system, I designed a small game in which you could join a lobby and select a role. That role would simply change the color of your capsule, which had a default first person controller. If someone was already the role you were trying to select, it would show that as well. To create a multiplayer game in which someone selects a role is great practice to expedite implementation in the final product.


    Cutscenes and UI/UX

    While I was working on the development of these smaller projects, I began to design some UI/UX aspects and commission some art by the same friend who did Inhuman. I have some of the early drafts of my concepts for the art already posted. I also started preproduction for a cutscene that will play before the game, which I decided would be in real life, writing a script and shot list.




    Next Steps

    In order to launch Space Toboggan successfully, I still need to formally announce the game, which I have been hesitant to do until I can devote some proper time to its completion. Once announced, I will do a fair amount of publicity for it within my budget constraints. After all three of the small games are done, which they are not (Chute is complete and development of Spinner is ongoing), I will implement the systems in the final product which will lay out a solid framework I can iterate on. At that point, a lot of production will be complete.


    One of the next big steps for the development of this project is to change the name.


    Prototype - Pinball


    My most recent game design venture is a small project called Pinball. The game is simple; get as high as possible. Pinball is meant to combine the Titanfall movement system with the Doodle Jump game design, where the character can jump up and to the side through a procedurally generated world. The game determines your score by your distance to the ground. Simple. I was a little ambitious in my timeframe when I was starting and realistically I could do it in maybe two to three weeks of actual development. Unfortunately, I was only able to put in about 5 days before school started again. That is the project I’m working on at the moment, and when I stream on Twitch that is what I’m working on.
