Writing


I've always had an interest in creative writing. When I was a kid, I spent much of my free time writing stories of varying quality but immense length. When the Covid-19 pandemic hit, I began to write with partners, manifesting myself as characters I wrote and spending hours, days, weeks, writing long stories about them. These characters would ultimately be the inspiration for what I animated later on, but developing them through stories and scripts I wrote by myself and with others would be especially important for building my game design knowledge. This character-centric style of writing and partner collaboration was a gigantic influence on how I would later design games. The games I write are always centered around the characters, the players, and how they can interact with these designed fictitous worlds.


Another big influence for me was Tabletop RPGs, or TTRPGs. Games like Dungeons and Dragons, Pathfinder, and especially GURPS are something that I've been playing for over a decade. I've been to conventions, designed homebrew for my characters, done the whole nine yards. I have dozens of D and D characters, but anyone can do that. What was important for me was when I applied the character format to other things. To the characters in the short stories I wrote. Breaking free of the rules of the system and creating my own, I wrote more character sheets than I could keep track of. But that quickly graduated into creating my own game system around what I knew.


Night City Legends is a text based RPG system that I wrote in inspiration from the Cyberpunk 2077 videogame (or at least what it was supposed to be) and the inspiration for the game, Cyberpunk 2020. The game is played on a Discord server that I created and had moderators for, but the important part of the server is the character creation system. Like most TTRPGs, Night City Legends revolves around building a character sheet with attributes and equipment and going on adventures in the world of Cyberpunk, making rolls for your abilities and installing cyberware to make your character more powerful. Each time you add augmentations to your character, though, you get one step closer to going insane, which is vitally important for the themes of madness and modding in the Cyberpunk IP. Night City, my character-driven RP system has been playtested to some extent and remains wildly fun.

Animation

Before I knew I wanted to make a game, I thought that the best way to tell some of the stories from one of the reoccuring worlds from my short stories would be through animations. Unfortunately, I had no idea how to do this. My first foray into digital storytelling was through Blender's 3D modeling program with a fully homemade and home-rigged model I called "Danny the Default Man", who I modeled a body and clothes for by hand (and it shows).


[Danny the Default Man Picture Sketches and Final]


Having completed my model after a few weeks of bashing my head into the wall, I decided to try animating this character. Unfortunately, it's now time for you to meet a good friend of mine.


[Marvin Picture]


This is Marvin. Marvin is my darling, my child, and my partner in crime. Unfortunately, Marvin does not really do so well when it comes to... performance, which is something that a computer might need when trying to render something like, I don't know, animations.


In trying to animate Danny the Default Man on his very first flight, I spent weeks wrestling with my computer's poor performance and my lack of skills in both animation and optimization at the time. The finished render was all of one frame per second (that is not a joke, the actual number hovered between about 0.8 and 1.1) and was a general tragedy. That being said, I learned a lot from this first animation, as you do when you're doing anything for the first time. I don't have that particular video any more, but ultimately I ended up doing a lot more animations to get my legs under me.


When I began work on Inhuman , I wanted to learn at least a little bit more animation for some of the gameplay. This prompted me to approach my Cartoon Animation teacher and ask if I could work on 3D animations in class, which she agreed to facilitate. I launched into a small spree of animations both in and out of class to hone my skills, to varying degrees of success. Rather than using my own assets, I switched to the default Adobe Y-Bot for most of my later animations because it came with a rig that was much more refined than my custom rigging job.


[Animation 1, 2, 3]


Some of the projects I've done were 24 hour challenges (though the time limitations were somewhat soft) where I would try to make the best animation possible within the set amount of time. This was especially convenient so I didn't spend too much time working on one thing and instead could move on to the next project (or homework). You can definitely see some of the performance issues in the computer and rendering.


[24 Hour renders and Postmortem]


I also spent some time working with static images and a bit of rendering to make sure I was, for the most part at least, not the cause of some of the rendering issues on other projects.

Computer Science


Computer Science. The life blood of video games. This may not have been the first way that I entered the game development cycle, but it quickly became the way that I want to continue up through the industry. When I first began to work on my project Inhuman , which became a bit of a flop for a variety of reasons, I spent a lot of my time coding features rather than working on things like animations, which had been something I was very interested in when I started the project. My focus on Computer Science in the last two and a half years has been explosive, and I'd like to walk through it a little bit.


When I was first writing text-based game systems on Discord , I thought it might be interesting to have a native command for many of the randomized functions that you would be doing in the game. Not finding any way to do this that already existed, I decided to create a Discord chatbot that could handle some of these functions. Calling it Nataya, the bot was split into two different entities, which were TempoBot (given that my handle on a fair amount of platforms is some variation on the name Tempo), the bot that essentially served as my "main" branch in Github terms, and the ScavengerBot entity, which was more in line with my "dev" branch. To undergo such a task, I asked for help from my father, a mathematician and general computer-science man. He walked me through some of the basics of Python, which was the language that the bot was written in. The project worked to some extent, and was able to communicate with Discord in some capacity. The design of the bot is still mostly documented, but to keep the bot active would require a server that I am not currently funding.


TempoBot functions were largely social, as that was what many of the people who were playtesting my system wanted at the time. The idea was essentially to maintain character sheets and system rules inside the bot and that way the system and characters could be distributed across a variety of different communities who might want to use the game system for their own purposes. The bot could create random names, character greetings, general character descriptors, and allow you to search "chests" for random drops.


[TempoBot and ScavengerBot Help Menu and Functions]


Another small project I worked on was very different in nature. I am a distance running athlete in both Track and Field and Cross Country, and while my sport doesn't require much more than a good pair of running shoes, I still found myself repeatedly frustrated by the nature of many runkeeper apps. In distance running, one of the more popular ways to train is by doing laps around the track at a specific pace with a specific rest. This takes a few different forms, sometimes in longer tempo runs, sometimes in something called VO2 max, but the formula itself is largely the same. The issue I encountered was simply human error; I always forgot to turn off my watch and check the time. So I thought "what if there was a way to automatically stop the watch when you finished your lap?" This launched a very quick design process of a Samsung smartwatch application that I called "Splitkeeper". Splitkeeper was written with an API called Tizen, which uses C# and is designed exclusively for Samsung smart-device development, like TVs and wearables, which serves me and my products which are primarily Samsung. Unfortunately, Tizen has the kind of documentation and functionality that makes it... lets say "known" among programmers, so the project has moved somewhat and as of right now is on indefinite hard-stop until my watch gets fixed. It was broken during a Cross Country meet and I have been unable to fix it as of right now.


[Splitkeeper Doc]

And finally, the biggest part of the learning curve for my very first project Inhuman were the computer science aspects. Given that the game revolved around a variety of abilities your character could use and systems to change these abilities as their first priority, most of what I did on that project was writing scripts. Inhuman was meant to be written in the Unity Engine, which revolves around C#, so I spent a lot of my time writing features for the game in C# rather than working on assets. Most of where I ended up with Inhuman, which in theory was going to be this masterpiece with high-end graphics and animations that I seemed to think would just happen, actually turned out to be a handwritten controller moving a cube across a small city set with a bunch of crazy abilities going off on button commands. Though the project ultimately was a failure, I learned a lot of C# and about the process of coding within the framework of a game engine. Design Documents are interesting. Lots of people keep a sketchbook, maybe for doodling and such, but the notes I keep are much less about the drawings and more about the notes themselves. I'm always thinking up new games, new stories, and new mechanics, which I draw everywhere. Everywhere as in actually everywhere, from an actual notebook to some of the Samsung notes to the Discord server I use to keep notes and images. One time I had nothing to write on as I was thinking about a movement system so I scrawled notes all over my thighs, which seemed like the largest canvas at the time. The Inhuman Story

When I was in sophomore year, I was well into the weeds of my animation journey. However, part of the curriculum for the International Baccalauriate Diploma Program that our school takes part in is that sophomores have to make what is called the "Personal Project". The IBDP Personal Project is an assignment that, quite frankly, is easy to get away with doing something easy. So what did I do?


Not that!


Lots of my friends took the easy way out. The amount of cookbooks that were made for the IBDP Personal Project was truly ridiculous. But 10th grade Connor Dolan, in his infinite wisdom, decided to capitalize on his animation skills that he had accrued over the 10th grade summer to make a video game based on some of the stories he had written over the COVID-19 pandemic. This game would be an open-world superhero game similar to Sucker Punch's inFamous Second Son on the PS4, which has been my favorite game since I first played it at a friend's house when I was way too young to be playing M rated games. I didn't really have any idea how to... yknow... build games, but I had the gifts of gumption and ignorance.


As I began to design, something I took very seriously for a project that should realistically have taken me about 10 hours, my imagination, which was already slipping away from my skills, really took off sprinting. I wanted to add multiplayer and high definition graphics and generally create the superhero game of my dreams. I spent my mandatory gym class writing on a massive design document, filling it with mechanics I wanted to add but had no idea how to implement. Every video I watched to try and learn how to build a game like this told me to simply not, but arrogance blinded me as I trudged forwards. Unity seemed the most accessible circa 2021, even if I didn't really understand the technical limitations of engines yet, so I spent a lot of time working in Unity. Rather than trying to come at the project from different angles and build up gradually, I stalwartly refused to take the "easy" (smart) way through the problem, which of course created problems. Messy projects, poorly written scripts, corrupt files, you name it, all of this happened during the Inhuman development project. Given that my grit had served me well during my nightmarish Covid-19 experience where I was forced to redo massive chunks of my work throughout the semester, I trekked on, mistaking the resolve it had taken to get through something outside of my control for the stubbornness it takes to not change course when the answer is clearly wrong. I simply assumed my "dedication" would pay off later on.


It didn't. My design document grew exponentially while my skills grew linearly. It made me question if I even wanted to be doing this, and while I learned a lot through the process, I ultimately limped across the 10th grade personal project finish line with a presentation of my design document. I had learned a lot, including C#, Blender, Mixamo and other Adobe products, and Unity, but the project I still consider one of my bigger failures.


[Inhuman Presentation]


The only thing that I think I did fairly well in terms of Inhuman was the design document. While the game suffered from a lot of core design issues, I will say that a fair amount of the game mechanics were interesting and fairly consistent. The game was meant to be about a character that was able to change their powers and abilities on the fly to suit the player's specific style of play. All of this was documented in a Google Site, which you can sort of see in the Presentation but I'll be sure to list down below. The design document also held some of the major locations that would come into play and a lot of miscellaneous design notes.


[Inhuman Game Mechanics]


The design document mostly held something that I had proven I was very good at, which was story. The Google Site held main story missions similar to that of inFamous, complete with character sheets and powers (movesets) for many of the enemies. These character sheets and designs were massive and detailed, but in the scheme of making an actual game were fairly useless. All told, the design document could be classified as a small novel.


[Inhuman Character Sheets]


While it was largely a failure, Inhuman was a phenomenal learning experience for me and ended up launching me into even more projects with renewed passion, hoping to one day be skilled enough to build Inhuman or something like it.
